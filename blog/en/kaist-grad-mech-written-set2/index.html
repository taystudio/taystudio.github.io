Set 2 (2nd session) — 5 problems

Q1. Math — Eigen value, Eigen vector

Three differential linear equations given (linear combination):

$$ \begin{cases} y_1'' = a\,y_1 + b\,y_2 + c\,y_3 \\ y_2'' = d\,y_1 + e\,y_2 + f\,y_3 \\ y_3'' = g\,y_1 + h\,y_2 + i\,y_3 \end{cases} $$

Expressed as a 3×3 matrix system $\mathbf{y}'' = A\mathbf{y}$, where

$$ A = \begin{pmatrix} a & b & c \\ d & e & f \\ g & h & i \end{pmatrix} $$

  • Solve the characteristic equation $\det(A - \lambda I) = 0$ for eigenvalues $\lambda$
  • Then find the eigenvalues $\lambda$ + corresponding eigenvectors $\mathbf{v}$ ($A\mathbf{v} = \lambda\mathbf{v}$)

⚠️ Scale is 3×3, so you need to be solid on the arithmetic procedure.

📌 Past trend — earlier exams asked about conservative fields. Recommend covering vector calculus end-to-end (independent path → Stokes' theorem).

Q2. Solid Mechanics — Statics, Hooke's Law, Principal Stress

Like Set 1, not a problem you solve in one shot. The analysis itself was easier than Set 1, but setup + execution takes time.

[Strength design procedure]

  1. Statics → Free-body diagram → solve for all reactant forces
  2. For composite structures, draw the Shear Force Diagram (SFD), Bending Moment Diagram (BMD) for the region of interest
  3. From SFD/BMD, compute each normal stress · shear stress in the chosen coordinate system (Torsion + Bending + Tension/Compression)
  4. At the point with max stress, compute principal stress: - 2D — Mohr's circle - 3D — find via eigenvectors (separate method)
  5. Use principal stress to compute factor of safety — or, with a fixed safety factor, the allowable max stress

💡 The problem I got involved a utility-pole-like configuration with a wire to prevent deflection. Assume the wire transmits no moment. Use the wire's strain to back out its tension.

🎯 Beyond strength design — stability-based design = Buckling. Fatigue etc. is covered separately in Material Behavior.

Q3. Dynamics — Rigid Body / Work & Energy

Not very different from Set 1. Two L-length bars fixed at a fixed point in a ㅗ (T) shape — only rotational DOF, no translation. Released from a specific position:

  • Mass moment of inertia
  • Maximum velocity
  • Maximum height it can reach (with proof)

💡 Solution — Work & Energy is cleanest.

Q4. Thermodynamics — Properties, T-v diagram

A deceptively simple problem — if you don't know basic thermodynamic properties, you can't solve it. No additional formula given.

[Problem]

A very large lake (initial state 25 °C, 1 atm) — drop 1 kg of hot water (100 °C) into it. What's the maximum work (energy) you can extract?

Given: h_fg, h_f, h_g, C (heat capacitance)

📚 Work through it with your thermo textbook.

Q5. Fluid Mechanics — Navier-Stokes (Couette flow)

Last one is Navier-Stokes, as expected.

[Couette flow problem]

Two different fluids in a Couette-flow setup — one plate fixed, the other moving. - Starting from the Navier-Stokes equation (Cartesian, Cylindrical) — assume laminar flow, prove unidirectional flow + continuity - Set boundary conditions + find the exact solution

📌 Fluid regular — Navier-Stokes. Cartesian dominates, but memorize the cylindrical form too.

📌 Three analysis types in fluid mechanics:

  • Control volume analysis (Reynolds transport theorem)
  • Finite differential analysis (Navier-Stokes equation)
  • Dimensional analysis

The first two are must-knows. Boundary layer / drag coefficient occasionally appear too.

That covers the second set.

I ended up rejected at the KAIST interview and went to my home university's grad program. To anyone reading this — study hard and good luck, and take care of your health :)
