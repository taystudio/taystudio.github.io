After my final undergrad semester wrapped, I finally got around to writing this. I was rejected at the KAIST 2nd-round interview, but the 1st-round written exam and 2nd-round interview experience might still be useful to anyone applying to KAIST Mechanical Engineering grad school.

I applied in July 2019, took the 1st-round exam on August 5, and the interview on August 20. I made it through the 1st round only.

1st Round — Passed

Figure 1. 1st-round written exam result

1st-Round Major Exam — Format / Subjects

Item Detail
Date 2019-08-05
Subjects General math (analysis, linear algebra), solid mechanics, dynamics, thermodynamics, fluid mechanics — 5 subjects
Duration 4 hours total
Sets Set 1 (2h) → 20-min break (snacks served) → Set 2 (2h)
Per-set composition One problem per subject = 5 problems per set
Grand total 2 sets × 5 problems = 10 problems

Difficulty note: Wide coverage, so study the major topics. Problem-level difficulty is roughly at the textbook example/exercise level on average. A few subjects require a bit more thought. Each problem has sub-questions (a)·(b)·(c)·(d) — anywhere from 3 to 5 sub-questions.

Set 1 (1st session) — 5 problems

Q1. Math — Basic ODE problem

A freshman-physics-style terminal velocity problem. Free body diagram → equation of motion → ODE.

  • No technique beyond separation of variables — but the acceleration term required separation applied twice.

📌 KAIST math regulars — ODE / PDE.

• ODE: cover the spectrum — from Exact differential equation to integrating factor methods.

• PDE: hand-solvable types are limited. At minimum know Separable Partial Differential Equations.

Q2. Solid Mechanics — Stress Analysis, Tensor, Factor of Safety

A bit on the harder side. The Von-Mises stress concept (covered in detail in Material Behavior) appeared.

  • Tensile yield stress given
  • Plug values into the stress equation given in polar coordinates → simplify → arrange to the term each sub-question asks for
  • The given equation was the Von-Mises (effective stress) form

📚 Essential: a solid grasp of stress (as a tensor) and the Tresca Criterion (Maximum shear stress criterion) from Material Behavior. With those, it's easy.

Q3. Dynamics — Rigid Body / Work & Energy

A square wheeled cart of mass m with a bar of mass m attached at the mass center. Released from angle θ:

  • (a) General angular velocity (formula)
  • (b) Maximum velocity during free fall
  • (c) Position at the final point

💡 Three dynamics approaches: (1) Equation of motion, (2) Work & Energy, (3) Impulse and momentum. Work & Energy is the cleanest here.

Q4. Thermodynamics — Energy equation, Isentropic process

I expected cycle analysis, but it came combined with fluid mechanics concepts.

Water falls from h1 → pipe → h2 (h1 > h2), passing through a turbine in between:

  • (a) Max energy (power) the turbine can extract
  • (b) Mass flow rate at that max power
  • (c) Required pipe cross-sectional area
  • (d) Entropy generated if isentropic efficiency is 0.6

💡 Solution — Bernoulli + Energy equation give the same result. Since we want the max, assume frictionless → Bernoulli applies.

Q5. Fluid Mechanics — Hydrostatic Force on a Curved Surface

Unexpectedly, hydrostatics. (Mech-eng doesn't usually drill hydrostatics deeply.)

A quarter-circle gate submerged in water: - Resultant horizontal/vertical force on the gate surface from the water - Locations of the horizontal/vertical centers of pressure - The reaction force at the pin

🎯 Lesson: Mech-eng courses don't drill hydrostatics much, but it can show up. Cover the major topics of each chapter.

The 5 problems above made up the first set — 2 hours.
